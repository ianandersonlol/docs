Here's the truth that makes you safe with this tool: **Claude is confident, and confidence is not the same as correctness.** It can be wrong, and when it's wrong it usually still *sounds* right. The skill that protects you isn't reading code — it's verifying *results*. You can do that with zero technical knowledge.

## "It ran without errors" ≠ "it's right"

A task can complete cleanly, produce a beautiful chart, and still be wrong — because Claude misread a column, made an assumption you didn't intend, or quietly dropped some rows. No error message will tell you that. Only checking will.

Treat every result as "probably right, let me confirm" rather than "done."

## Six ways to verify (no code required)

<Steps>
  <Step title="Spot-check against a known answer">
    Pick one thing you can verify by hand and check it. If the report says March revenue was \$48,200, find March yourself — or ask Claude to show the rows behind it — and confirm.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Ask Claude to explain in plain English">
    > Explain what you just did, step by step, as if I'm not technical.

    If the explanation reveals an assumption you don't like ("I treated blank amounts as zero"), you've found a problem the output alone would have hidden.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Make it show its work">
    > Which exact rows did you include in the March total? List them.

    Numbers should be traceable back to the source. If Claude can't show where a figure came from, don't trust the figure.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Have it check itself">
    > Double-check your own work and tell me where you might be wrong or what assumptions you made.

    Claude is often good at catching its own mistakes when explicitly asked to look for them.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Sanity-check the totals">
    Do the big numbers pass the smell test? Does the cleaned file still have a sensible number of rows? A total that's 10× too big or a month that vanished is a flag worth chasing.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Check whether the input was readable">
    A wrong answer often starts with a file Claude could not read cleanly. For PDFs and spreadsheets, ask: *"Which files or sheets were hard to read, and what did you skip?"* See [Files Claude May Struggle With](/agentic-ai/claude-code/first-session/file-limitations).
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Red flags that mean "slow down"

- The result is **surprising** in a way you can't explain. Surprise is information — investigate before you celebrate.
- Numbers **don't reconcile** (the parts don't add up to the whole).
- Claude made a **judgment call** you weren't asked about ("I assumed the fiscal year starts in April").
- A file got **much smaller or much bigger** than you expected.
- Claude sounds **unusually hedgy** ("this should roughly work"). Take the hint.

## Match your scrutiny to the stakes

Not everything needs a forensic audit. Calibrate:

| Stakes | How hard to check |
|---|---|
| Throwaway exploration, just for you | A glance is fine |
| Something you'll share internally | Spot-check the key numbers |
| A decision, a client, money, or the public | Verify thoroughly; trace every figure that matters |

<Warning>
  The higher the stakes, the more it matters that *you* — not Claude — are the final check. Claude is a brilliant assistant, not an accountable one. You're accountable for what you ship.
</Warning>
