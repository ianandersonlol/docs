A static model is a report. A model you can *interrogate* is a decision tool. This is where the project pays off: with the forecast built and your [conventions captured in a skill](/agentic-ai/claude-code/forecasting-model/write-a-skill), you can now ask the kinds of questions you'd normally spend an afternoon wiring up in Excel — and get answers in a sentence.

**What you'll practice:** treating the model as something you *converse with*, and verifying every answer before you believe it.

## Two ways to ask — know the difference

When you ask a question, you can have Claude either **answer in the chat** or **change the model and re-export**. They're different tools:

| You want… | Ask for… | Example |
|---|---|---|
| A quick read, model untouched | An answer in the chat | "What's ending ARR in the downside case?" |
| A new saved version to share | An update to `forecast.xlsx` + fresh outputs | "Add a 7% churn scenario and re-export the summary." |

<Tip>
  Be explicit about which you want. *"Just tell me — don't change the file"* keeps the model frozen. *"Update the model and save it as a new dated copy"* produces an artifact. Mixing these up is the most common source of "wait, why did my model change?"
</Tip>

## The questions worth asking

<Steps>
  <Step title="Driver questions — what's moving the numbers">
    > In the base case, what's driving the revenue ramp more — new customer adds or rising revenue per customer? Break down the growth between the two.

    This is the analysis you actually care about: not *what* the forecast says, but *why*.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Sensitivities — how fragile is it">
    > Hold everything else flat. Show me ending ARR if monthly churn is 3%, 4%, 5%, and 6%. How much ARR does each point of churn cost me?

    One prompt, a sensitivity table. Ask for the ones that keep you up at night — churn, new-customer pace, price.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Goal-seek — work backwards from a target">
    > I want to hit $2M ARR by December next year. Holding churn and price at base, how many new customers per month would I need? Is that realistic versus our recent actuals?

    This is the question executives actually ask, and the one spreadsheets make tedious. Here it's a sentence.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Scenario comparison — line them up">
    > Put base, upside, and downside side by side: ending customers, ending ARR, total 24-month revenue, and ending cash. Which scenario runs out of cash, and when?

    A clean comparison table you can drop straight into a board deck.
  </Step>

  <Step title="A custom what-if — save this one">
    > Build a fourth scenario: we raise price 10% in month 6 but it lifts churn by one point. Net effect on ending ARR versus base? If it's positive, add it to the model as a new scenario and save a dated copy.

    Notice this one crosses the line from *asking* to *changing* — so it gets a dated backup, per your skill.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Verify every answer

A confident wrong answer is the whole danger of this step. The model can be audited, so audit it.

- **Make it show its work.** *"Show me the exact cells and formula behind that $2M ARR figure."* Numbers you can trace are numbers you can trust.
- **Sanity-check the direction.** Higher churn should *lower* ARR. If a sensitivity moves the wrong way, a sign is flipped somewhere — stop and dig.
- **Reconcile against the file.** *"Does the number you just told me match what's actually in `forecast.xlsx` right now?"* This catches answers computed in the chat that never made it into the model (or vice versa).
- **Spot-check one by hand.** Take the 5% churn sensitivity and check a single month's roll-forward with a calculator. One match buys a lot of confidence.

<Warning>
  Claude will happily answer a question the model can't actually support — extrapolating, assuming, or quietly inventing an input. Always ask *"what did you assume to answer that?"* For anything headed to an investor or your CFO, trace it back to a cell. See [Reviewing &amp; Verifying](/agentic-ai/claude-code/best-practices/reviewing-and-verifying).
</Warning>

## You're now running the model, not just building it

Building the model was step one; this is the part that makes it worth having. But you've probably noticed a rhythm forming — new actuals arrive, you refresh, you re-run the same handful of questions, you re-export the same outputs. That repetition is the signal it's time to wrap the whole routine into one command.
