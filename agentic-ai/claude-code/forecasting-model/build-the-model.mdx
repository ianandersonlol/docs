You've got the [project folder](/agentic-ai/claude-code/forecasting-model) and the historical actuals in `data/`. Now you'll turn those 18 months of history into a 24-month forward forecast — driven by a handful of assumptions you control, built on formulas you can audit.

**What you'll practice:** the [core loop](/agentic-ai/claude-code/first-session/core-loop) on a project that has to be *correct*, plan-first discipline, and verifying a model against reality.

## The mindset: drivers, not magic

A good forecast isn't a guess about next month's revenue. It's a small set of **drivers** — new customers per month, churn rate, average revenue per customer, costs — and the math that turns those drivers into revenue and cash. Change a driver, the whole forecast moves. That's the point: you want a model you can *steer*, not a static chart.

So your first job isn't "predict the future." It's "let's agree on the drivers, then let the spreadsheet do the arithmetic."

## The walkthrough

<Steps>
  <Step title="Let Claude read the history first">
    Before forecasting anything, have Claude understand the past — exactly like the [CSV project](/agentic-ai/claude-code/first-projects/csv-to-chart):

    > Look at `data/monthly-actuals.csv`. Tell me what's in it, then calculate the trends that matter for a forecast: the average monthly new-customer count, the monthly churn rate (churned ÷ starting customers), and the average revenue per customer (MRR ÷ customers). Show me how each has moved over the 18 months.

    Now you both know the starting point — and you've learned the drivers' recent values instead of inventing them.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Ask for a plan and an assumptions block — not the model yet">
    This is the key move. Get the structure agreed *before* a single formula is written:

    > Plan a 24-month forecast that continues from the last actual month. I want it **driver-based**: a clearly labeled **assumptions block** at the top — new customers per month, monthly churn rate, revenue per customer, gross margin %, and fixed monthly operating costs — and then the forecast reading from those assumptions. Propose starting values based on the recent trends, but don't build it yet. Show me the plan and the assumptions you'd use.

    Claude proposes the layout and starting assumptions. Read it like an analyst reviewing a junior's setup — adjust anything that looks off ("use 5% churn, not 6%"; "hold price flat").
  </Step>

  <Step title="Insist on live formulas">
    Before it builds, set the one rule that makes the model trustworthy:

    > Build it in `model/forecast.xlsx`. Every forecast cell must be a **live Excel formula that references the assumptions block** — no hard-coded numbers in the forecast. I want to change an assumption and watch the whole model update.

    This is the difference between a real model and a screenshot. Hard-coded numbers can't be steered or audited; formulas can.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Let it build, approving as it goes">
    Claude creates the spreadsheet, asking permission to write the file. When it finishes, it'll summarize the structure: an assumptions block up top, then monthly rows for customers, MRR, ARR, revenue, gross profit, operating costs, and cash/EBITDA.
  </Step>

  <Step title="Add the scenarios">
    Now make it flex:

    > Add a scenario selector with three presets — **base**, **upside**, **downside** — that change the new-customer and churn assumptions. Upside: more new customers, lower churn. Downside: the reverse. I want to switch scenarios in one place and have the forecast recalculate.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Verify before you trust it

This is non-negotiable. A forecast that reaches your CFO is *your* forecast, not Claude's — so check it like a skeptic.

- **Tie to actuals.** *"Does the first forecast month continue cleanly from the last actual? Show me the hand-off — last actual customers and MRR vs. the first forecast row."* A discontinuity here means a broken assumption.
- **Audit one column's formula.** Open `forecast.xlsx` in Excel and click a forecast cell. Is it a formula pointing at the assumptions block, or a typed-in number? Spot-check MRR: does it equal customers × revenue-per-customer?
- **Trace the customer roll-forward.** *"Walk me through how customers go from one month to the next — which cells add new customers and subtract churn?"* It should be: prior customers + new − (prior customers × churn rate).
- **Stress one driver.** Change new-customers-per-month to zero in the assumptions and confirm the forecast actually flattens. If nothing moves, the formulas aren't live — send it back.

<Warning>
  "It produced a model" is not "the model is right." The most common silent failure is **hard-coded forecast cells that look like formulas but aren't** — they'll never respond to your scenarios. Thirty seconds clicking cells in Excel is what makes this trustworthy. See [Reviewing &amp; Verifying](/agentic-ai/claude-code/best-practices/reviewing-and-verifying).
</Warning>

## Make it presentable

Once the math checks out, ask for the outputs you'd actually share:

> Save a chart of monthly revenue across all three scenarios to `outputs/revenue-scenarios.png`, and a one-page summary table of ending ARR, total revenue, and ending cash by scenario.

## What you have now

A working, auditable, driver-based model in `model/forecast.xlsx` that you can steer with assumptions and flip between scenarios. That's already more than most people build in a week.

But notice what just happened: you spent several careful prompts teaching Claude *how you like a model built* — formulas not hard-codes, a labeled assumptions block, a scenario selector. Next month you'd have to say all of that again. Unless you capture it once.
